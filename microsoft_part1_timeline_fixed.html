üë¶ PRE-FOUNDING: THE ORIGINS (1955-1975)
1955-1968
Bill Gates Early Years
Bill Gates, Mary Maxwell Gates, William Gates Sr.
Born into Seattle privilege. Father: prominent lawyer. Mother: UW regent, United Way exec, served on boards with IBM CEO. Competitive household - everything was a contest. Bill read entire World Book Encyclopedia as a child.
Why It Matters: The connections, especially Mary Gates' relationship with IBM leadership, would prove decisive. The competitive drive would define Microsoft's culture.
1968
Lakeside School Computer Club
Bill Gates (8th grade), Paul Allen (10th grade), Ric Weiland, Kent Evans
Elite Seattle prep school gets teletype terminal to GE mainframe. Gates and Allen become obsessed, spending every available hour programming. School's $3,000 computer budget exhausted in weeks.
Why It Matters: One of the only high schools in America with computer access. This 2-year head start on 99.99% of their generation proved crucial.
1970-1971
Traf-O-Data: The Failed First Company
Bill Gates (16), Paul Allen (18)
Built traffic counter analysis system using Intel 8008 processor. Made ~$20,000 but company ultimately failed. Key lesson: they learned to program microprocessors when almost no one knew they existed.
Why It Matters: Failure taught them microprocessors would change everything. "Success is a lousy teacher. It seduces smart people into thinking they can't lose."
January 1975
Popular Electronics: Altair 8800 Cover
Ed Roberts (MITS), Paul Allen spots it
"World's First Minicomputer Kit to Rival Commercial Models" - $439 kit computer. Paul Allen sees it at newsstand, runs to show Bill at Harvard. They realize: microcomputers need software, and nobody else knows how to write it.
Why It Matters: THE moment. They see the future and realize they're the only ones positioned to capture it.
üöÄ FOUNDING ERA: ALBUQUERQUE (1975-1979)
February 1975
BASIC for Altair: The Bluff
Bill Gates (19), Paul Allen (22)
Without ever seeing an Altair, they call MITS claiming they have BASIC ready. Gates drops out of Harvard, codes for 8 weeks straight. Allen flies to demo - it works on first try! First sale: $3,000 + royalties.
Why It Matters: Microsoft's first product. Established pattern: move fast, sell first, build later. "We set the DOS prompt to A> instead of > to make it look different from CP/M"
April 4, 1975
Microsoft Founded
Bill Gates (19), Paul Allen (22)
Partnership: Gates 60%, Allen 40% (later adjusted to 64/36 because "Bill did more work on BASIC"). Name = Microcomputer + Software. Vision: "A computer on every desk and in every home."
Why It Matters: The beginning. When IBM had 340,000 employees and $23B revenue, Microsoft had 2 people and a borrowed computer.
1976
Open Letter to Hobbyists
Bill Gates
"Most of you steal your software... One thing you do is prevent good software from being written. Who can afford to do professional work for nothing?" Published in Homebrew Computer Club newsletter, causes uproar.
Why It Matters: Created the concept of commercial software. Before this, software was free. Gates invented the business model that created a $500B industry.
1977-1978
BASIC Everywhere Strategy
Bill Gates, Paul Allen
License BASIC to every computer manufacturer: Apple II, Commodore PET, TRS-80, plus 50+ others. Revenue hits $1.3M by 1978. Microsoft BASIC becomes the de facto standard.
Why It Matters: First glimpse of the strategy: become the standard, then leverage that position. "We want to be on every machine."
üíº THE IBM ERA & DOS DOMINANCE (1980-1985)
July 1980
IBM Visits Microsoft
IBM (Project Chess team), Bill Gates, Steve Ballmer
IBM secretly building PC, needs programming languages. They expect hippies, find businessmen. Gates' mom Mary had served on United Way board with IBM CEO John Opel - "Mary Gates' boy".
Why It Matters: The most important meeting in business history begins. IBM doesn't know they're about to hand the keys to the kingdom to a 24-year-old.
November 1980
THE IBM DEAL - The Greatest Business Deal Ever
Bill Gates, IBM, Tim Paterson (Seattle Computer)
Microsoft licenses 86-DOS for $25K, then licenses it to IBM as PC-DOS. KEY TERMS: (1) Microsoft keeps ownership (2) Per-copy royalty not flat fee (3) Can license to others (4) IBM pays $430K for development
Why It Matters: THE DEAL THAT CREATED THE MODERN TECH INDUSTRY. IBM thought they were buying an OS. They actually transferred their monopoly power to Microsoft. "IBM playing checkers while Bill played chess."
August 1981
IBM PC Launch
IBM, Microsoft
IBM PC ships with PC-DOS 1.0. Instant success - 241,683 units in first year. Every PC says "Microsoft BASIC" on boot. Microsoft revenue jumps to $16M.
Why It Matters: Microsoft now riding IBM's distribution. Every IBM PC sold makes Microsoft stronger. The beginning of the end for IBM's dominance.
1982-1983
The Compaq Moment - MS-DOS Licensed
Rod Canion (Compaq), Bill Gates
Compaq creates first IBM PC clone, needs DOS. Microsoft licenses MS-DOS to them. Then to HP, Dell, Gateway, everyone. By 1983: 30+ PC manufacturers using MS-DOS.
Why It Matters: The trap springs. IBM created the market, but Microsoft captures all the value. Every PC maker needs MS-DOS to be "IBM compatible."
1983
Paul Allen Leaves
Paul Allen, Bill Gates
Paul diagnosed with Hodgkin's lymphoma. Overhears Bill and Steve Ballmer discussing diluting his equity. Resigns but keeps 28% stake. Relationship permanently strained.
Why It Matters: End of founding partnership. Bill now unchallenged leader. Paul's stake worth $100+ billion today.
November 1983
Windows Announced
Bill Gates
Seeing Apple's Macintosh GUI, Gates announces Windows. Ships late 1985, terrible ("tank on a Volkswagen"), but doesn't matter - it's the IBM-compatible GUI.
Why It Matters: Microsoft now attacking on two fronts: DOS for today, Windows for tomorrow. Apple can't license Mac OS, sealing their fate.

üìä APPLICATIONS DOMINANCE (1985-1990)
1985
Excel for Mac: The Trojan Horse
Bill Gates, Mac team
Microsoft releases Excel for Macintosh FIRST, before PC version. Superior to Lotus 1-2-3. Strategy: target the next platform (GUI) while competitor dominates current one (DOS).
Why It Matters: The Lotus killer. Shows Microsoft's strategic genius: don't fight on enemy's turf, wait for platform shift. Excel becomes #1 when Windows succeeds.
March 1986
IPO - Going Public
Bill Gates, Goldman Sachs
IPO at $21/share, raising $61M. Bill worth $350M at age 30, youngest self-made billionaire ever. 12,000% return if held to today.
Why It Matters: Capital for expansion. Currency for acquisitions. Legitimacy with enterprises. The war chest for the coming battles.
1987
Windows 2.0 + Excel PC
Bill Gates, dev teams
Windows 2.0 ships with Excel and Word. Still not great, but good enough. Lotus refuses to build for Windows, thinking it's a toy.
Why It Matters: The applications make the platform. Microsoft now has OS AND killer apps. Lotus makes fatal mistake ignoring Windows.
1989
Office Suite Bundle
Bill Gates, marketing team
Bundles Word, Excel, PowerPoint (acquired) as "Microsoft Office" for $750 vs $1,500 separately. Competitors can't match - they only have one product each.
Why It Matters: The bundle that killed competition. By 1991, Office generates more revenue than Windows. The second monopoly.
May 1990
Windows 3.0 - Finally Good
Bill Gates, Brad Silverberg
Windows 3.0 ships - first truly usable version. Sells 10 million copies in 2 years. Lotus finally builds for Windows - too late, Excel already won.
Why It Matters: The tipping point. Windows goes from joke to juggernaut. GUI computing goes mainstream. Microsoft now unstoppable.
üí∞ 1990: $1.18 billion revenue ‚Ä¢ 5,600 employees ‚Ä¢ Windows + Office = dominance
üëë TOTAL DOMINATION (1991-1995)
1991-1992
Enterprise Sales Force
Steve Ballmer
Ballmer builds enterprise sales force from scratch. Sells to Fortune 500 IT departments who hate PCs (mainframe guys). Creates site licensing.
Why It Matters: Conquers the enterprise. Not just consumers and small business anymore - now the Fortune 500. Recurring revenue model.
1992
Windows 3.1
Brad Silverberg, dev team
Windows 3.1 ships with multimedia support. Sells 3 million copies in first 2 months. Pre-installed on most PCs. Network effects fully kicked in.
Why It Matters: Windows becomes THE platform. Developers only build for Windows. Users only buy Windows. The monopoly is complete.
1993-1994
Windows 95 Development ("Chicago")
Brad Silverberg, dev team, Bill Gates
Next-gen Windows under development: 32-bit OS, better multitasking, plug-and-play, built-in networking, Internet integration (late addition), consumer-friendly. Team laid out principles, pushed responsibility down. Less bureaucracy. Everyone felt personally responsible.
Why It Matters: Biggest software launch in history coming. Building technical product AND consumer brand simultaneously.
1994
Antitrust Pressure
DOJ, Bill Gates
DOJ investigates Microsoft for anticompetitive practices, particularly per-processor licensing (1988-1994). Microsoft agrees to stop in consent decree.
Why It Matters: First serious regulatory scrutiny. Antitrust battles just beginning (won't fully resolve until 2001+).
üé∏ August 24, 1995
WINDOWS 95 LAUNCH - THE CULTURAL MOMENT
Bill Gates, Jay Leno, Brad Silverberg, Rolling Stones
$300 MILLION MARKETING CAMPAIGN
Launch event: Jay Leno, Rolling Stones "Start Me Up" ($8-14M licensing), massive TV/print ads, Empire State Building in Windows colors, midnight retail parties worldwide.

The Product: Start button & taskbar (still used today), 32-bit OS, plug-and-play, TCP/IP networking, consumer-friendly interface.
Why It Matters - THE CULMINATION: Windows becomes CONSUMER BRAND. Not just business software - culturally significant. Stores sell out. Midnight lines. Marketing OS like blockbuster movie. Completes dominance of consumer AND enterprise. 7M copies in 5 weeks.

üîë STRATEGIC INSIGHTS & LESSONS

The IBM Deal - The Pivot Point

The single most important business decision in tech history. Microsoft negotiated to (1) Keep DOS ownership, (2) License per-copy not flat fee, (3) License to IBM competitors. Result: IBM's distribution created demand for DOS, Microsoft captured all value. "IBM played checkers while Bill played chess."

The Positive Spiral / Network Effects

Bill understood: "Success reinforces success in growing markets." Slight advantage in high-tech products with high volume and low marginal cost compounds. Make it no-brainer to use your platform ‚Üí becomes standard ‚Üí developers target it ‚Üí more users ‚Üí more developers ‚Üí unstoppable.

Always Target the Next Platform

The Lotus 1-2-3 lesson: Don't compete on current technology. Target NEXT platform. That's why Excel for Mac first (GUI was future), even though Mac tiny. Counter-positioning: wait for next technology generation.

OEM Leverage (The Visa Model)

Microsoft scaled through OEM partnerships like Visa scaled through banks. Tiny team (20 people) reached billions through Compaq, Dell, HP, Gateway. Distribution without building distribution. Open network effects.

Enterprise Requires Different Motion

Steve Ballmer gets 100% credit. Required: learning to sell to non-technical C-suite, dealing with IT departments who hated PCs, site licensing models, support infrastructure, server products. Completely different from OEM consumer sales.

International = Strategic, Not Secondary

Go global early. High-quality localization. Give local teams autonomy. Scale economies: amortize dev costs over global base. Network effects: become the standard everywhere. They realized most companies did terrible localization and made it strategic.

Talent Magnet Culture

In PC era, if you were ambitious - technical, sales, or marketing - Microsoft was THE place. People worked half to death but remember it as "the magic" and "good old days." Everyone felt personally responsible for products.

Fast Follower / Shameless Copier

Microsoft not first with ANYTHING (spreadsheets, word processing, GUI, OS). No shame copying good ideas. Better risk-adjusted returns - you know what works before shipping. Excel included Lotus keyboard shortcuts to ease switching.

Software Business Model Invention

Before Microsoft: software was free, bundled, or academic. After Microsoft: software is licensed per-copy. Near-zero marginal cost but high dev cost. Bill's "Open Letter to Hobbyists" created entire software industry business model.
